I remember the cold more than anything else.

It was a Saturday in November, the kind of morning where your breath fogs in front of your face before the market stalls are even up. I had a fold-out trestle table, a bolt of unbleached linen, and a single jar of granola — six hundred grams of toasted oats, smoked almonds, raw buckwheat, and wild honey from a hive in Herefordshire.

I had written the ingredients on a card in my best handwriting. *Toasted oat. Smoked almond. Wild honey.* Nothing else.

By eleven o'clock, the jar was gone.

---

## Why That Morning Matters

I did not understand it at the time, but that cold November morning contained everything Golden Batch has tried to be ever since: one thing, made well, from ingredients that had a story. No packaging spin. No nutritional claims. Just grain, fat, sweetness, and heat, in the right proportions at the right moment in the season.

The woman who bought that first jar came back the following week. She told me it was the best granola she had ever eaten. I thanked her and nearly said it was probably a fluke.

It wasn't a fluke. It was the recipe working.

---

## What I Have Learned Since

In eight years of baking, I have made exactly four mistakes that produced genuinely inedible results. All four came from the same error: rushing. Using an ingredient before it was ready — a batch of almonds that had not fully dried after blanching, a honey that crystallised too quickly in the oven because I had added it too early, a fig that was beautiful on the outside and underripe in the centre.

The lesson is obvious. The execution is harder than it looks.

> *We bake when the ingredients are right. You'll know when to expect us.*

I wrote that line for the website because it is true, but also because I needed to commit to it publicly. It is easy to bake on a schedule. It is harder to wait for the season to catch up.

## The Autumn 2026 Batch

This autumn, we are working with black figs from a single orchard in Almería, slow-toasted whole oats from Ballyhoura Mountain Grains in County Limerick, and clove pressed in micro-measure — barely there, just a warmth that sits underneath everything else like a held note.

It is, I think, the most considered batch we have ever made. Not the most complex — the complexity is there on purpose, but it should feel effortless to eat. The measure of a good batch is whether you notice the individual flavours or simply notice that it is right.
